CV Boots and Mold

Skills Gained

CAD Modeling DFM/DFA FDM 3d Printing Mold Design/Pouring

Description

Our Formula SAE car had a huge problem -- the suspension joints ran too close to the tripod housings and the half shafts. No off the shelf CV Boots would be able to fit without rubbing, which is a huge problem, as not only would the car not be rules legal, but the half shaft joints also wouldn't be protected, leading to catastrophic failure.

Summary

The solution was to design our own CV Boots, with custom geometry. After looking through 4 different options, we settled on using PMC 770, which has a 30 minute pouring time and a durometer of 70A.

Another problem came in the mold; since the boots were ribbed and hollow, there had to be two parts to the mold minimum: an inner and an outer mold. However, the ribs made it impossible to remove the inner mold, unless it was destroyed and reprinted each time.

We solved this by dividing the inner mold into different sections, that would slide out and collapse to barely be able to fit and remove. After iterating over tens of versions, we finally settled on a CV Boot geometry and mold design that had 6 pieces, which all fit together and made our CV Boot.

Pouring was also a big difficulty: with the time constriant of a 10 minute working time, we had to work fast, and after many many attempts, we settled on a formula of around 5-6 minutes of degassing in a vaccuum chamber, and then tilting the mold while pouring, which we found resulted in the most success and the least amount of bubbles. There's a lot more that we learned through trial and error; however, in the end, our innovative solution successfully sealed our CV joint and scored us a bonus point at the FSAE competition.
