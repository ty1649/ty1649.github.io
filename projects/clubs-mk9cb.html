CV Boots and Mold

Skills Gained

CAD Modeling DFM/DFA FDM 3d Printing Mold Design/Pouring

Description

Situation/Task:Our Formula SAE car had a big issue: the suspension joints were way too close to the tripod housings and half shafts. No off-the-shelf CV Boots would fit without rubbing, which was a major problem. Not only would the car not be rules legal, but the half shaft joints wouldn't be protected, leading to potential disaster.

Summary

Action: We decided to design our own CV Boots with custom geometry. First, for material; after checking out four different options, we went with PMC 770 because it has a 30-minute pouring time and a durometer of 70A, better than the other options. The mold was tricky since the boots were ribbed and hollow, requiring at least two parts: an inner and an outer mold. The ribs made it impossible to remove the inner mold unless it was destroyed and reprinted each time. I solved this by dividing the inner mold into sections that could slide out and collapse for easy removal. After iterating over many versions, I finally settled on a CV Boot geometry and mold design with six pieces that fit together perfectly, and 3d printed them at our makerspace. Pouring was also challenging due to the 10-minute working time, but after many attempts, we found success with a formula of 5-6 minutes of degassing in a vacuum chamber and tilting the mold while pouring to minimize bubbles.

Result: Our innovative solution successfully sealed the CV joint, making the car rules legal and preventing mechanical failure. Plus, we scored a bonus point at the FSAE competition!
