Xenopus Embryo Sorter Design

Skills Gained

Microfluidics SLA Printing FDM Printing PDMS Casting Hardware Integration

Problem Statement

As a Research Intern at Stanford University's Beel Research Group, I needed to design and manufacture hardware components for a computer vision-based Xenopus embryo sorter. The system needed to efficiently sort 50 eggs per second with 99% accuracy, requiring precise microfluidics design and careful integration of imaging components.

My Work

Led the development of the microfluidics and hardware systems:

  • Designed an innovative PDMS-based microfluidics chip featuring a serpentine flow channel for controlled embryo movement
  • Integrated a quake valve sorter mechanism to enable precise and rapid embryo sorting
  • Created and optimized molds using SLA printing technology for precise PDMS chip casting
  • Developed multiple iterations of FDM-printed housings to protect and align chip and electrical components
  • Engineered the system to handle millimeter-wide Xenopus embryos while maintaining quick and accurate imaging capabilities

Results

The embryo sorter project achieved several key milestones:

  • Successfully designed a microfluidics system capable of handling delicate Xenopus embryos
  • Achieved target throughput of 50 eggs per second
  • Maintained 99% sorting accuracy through precise flow control and imaging alignment
  • Created reliable and reproducible PDMS chip casting process
  • Developed modular housing design allowing for easy maintenance and component replacement

Lessons Learned

This project provided valuable insights into:

  • Design considerations for microfluidics systems handling biological specimens
  • Integration of multiple manufacturing processes (SLA, FDM, PDMS casting)
  • Optimization of flow channels for precise specimen handling
  • Balance between speed and accuracy in automated sorting systems
  • Importance of modular design in research equipment
